The Circle of Fifths: A Tool for Beginner Guitarists

The guitar is one instrument that draws attention to anyone wielding it with the right intention. Every experienced guitarist has their own story of how they got started (including tips and tricks). One tip most guitarists share in their guitar-playing origin tale is the Circle of Fifths.

As a new guitarist, navigating the fretboard can seem like serious finger aerobics. Everyone learns at different paces; some may find certain things a bit more challenging than others. With that said, every great guitarist practiced their way to their position. It means you can too!

There are several tricks, patterns, and tools for beginner guitarists. You can use these to help familiarise you with how guitars work. The Circle of Fifths is one such tool. Let's look at what it is, how it works, and how you can use it on your guitar.

What Is The Circle of Fifths?

To put it as simply as possible, the Circle of Fifths builds chromatic scales from the fifth note of the initial (starting) or previous scale, using the same chromatic principles. In other words, it organizes the 12 music notes into cycles of perfect fifths.

As a standard practice, the Circle of Fifths starts on the C major scale. It is the only scale with no sharp notes or flat notes. The Circle of Fifths arranges notes in a circle and places them in order of key signature.

For example, the Cmajor scale has no sharps or flats, so it is the first scale on the round chart. The following scale has one sharp note, the next has two, and so on until we've come back full circle.

How the Circle of Fifths Works

The key to understanding the Circle of Fifths as a new guitarist is to understand the principles of chromatic scales (or major/minor scales). For the sake of keeping the brain unfried, we'll use the C major scale to explain chromatic scales.

Chromatic Scales and the Circle of Fifths

Scales are sonic note groups consisting of eight notes. Each scale starts and ends on the same note, an octave higher or lower if played from the top down. Chromatic scales use the following tone and semitone sequence:

C D E F G A B C
T T S T T T S

This may seem like a bunch of random letters, but fret not, here's what they mean: Your scale has tones (T) and semitones (S). These are the difference between each note. You move a tone higher to get from C to D. You move another tone up to get from D to E. Because there is no full note between E and F, you move a semitone up.

The C major scale is the most commonly used in music. It's a chromatic scale that combines tones and semitones that arrange the 12 notes into groups of eight notes. The C major scale consists of the following notes:

C D E F G A B C

The lower C and higher C are octaves of each other. According to the principles of the Circle of Fifths, our next note on the chart is G. The G major scale has one sharp note, F#. This makes it the next relative key signature to C major. It follows clockwise on the chart. The scale of G major has the following notes:

G A B C D E F# G

The following note on our Circle of Fiths, the fifth note from G, is D. The D major scale has two sharp notes and follows clockwise after G. It keeps the F# from our G major scale and adds a second one, C#. The D major scale has the following notes:

D E F# G A B C# D

The fifth note in the D major scale is A. A major has three sharp notes, including the previous two, F# and C#. It has G# as its third sharp note. The A major scale follows clockwise on our Circle of Fifths chart and has the following notes:

A B C# D E F# G# A

The next note in the Circle of Fifths is E. It's the fifth note in the A major scale and follows clockwise after it.

This sequence gets followed until you reach your starting point again, C major. The Circle of Fifths is a great tool because it works for minor keys too. The difference is that it starts with the A minor scale. The A minor scale, like the C major scale, has no sharp or flat notes. The next note for our minor scales would be E minor. E is the fifth note from the A minor scale.

How To Use the Circle of Fifths on Guitar

The Circle of Fifths is a map of how the different notes on your guitar relate to each other. It's a great tool to help you learn your fretboard and how to find keys. Standard guitar tuning includes the following strings and notes (from the bottom up):

Each fret is a semitone up from your root note. You can apply the Circle of Fifths to find different notes, song keys, and chords on your guitar.

For example, if you practice playing to blues or have heard people talking about blues music, you may have heard the phrase, “1, 4, 5”. This is the shorthand for the song's chord progression. If the song starts in C major (1) the fourth and fifth chords in our C major scale would be F and G major.

Looking at the Circle of Fifths chart, the note to the left of the C major is F, and the key to the right of the C major is G. We can use the Circle of Fifths to find the 1, 4, and 5 chords of any natural major or minor key.

It is also great for transposing. If you have to change the key of a song, the Circle of Fifths is a fantastic way of finding the relative chords of the music in your new key.

Beginner guitarists can use the Circle of Fifths to find relative minors. Relative minor chords share the same scales as their related major chords. The relative minor for C major is A minor, written underneath. It goes for all the major and minor notes on the chart.

The Circle of Fifths is essential if you want to compose your own music. It shows you which chords work well together, which chords are related, which chord progressions work best together, and how to arrange the progressions.

The Circle of Fifths is an essential tool for beginner guitarists and musicians. It's a quick way to navigate your major and minor chords and learn notes and how they relate to each other.

Mastering your instrument requires commitment and practice. Your journey to being an incredible guitarist can be a little easier with the right tools. This guide aims to simplify the concept of the Circle of Fifths for you to get one step closer to your guitar-shredding dream.
